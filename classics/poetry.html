The Iliad by Homer (800 B.C.)

The Iliad is an ancient Greek epic poem written by Homer in the 8th century BC. It tells the story of the Trojan War and the battle between the Trojan prince Hector and the Greek warrior Achilles. The war was fought over the beautiful Helen, the wife of the Spartan king Menelaus, who was abducted by the Trojan prince Paris.

The poem begins with the Greeks, who have been fighting the Trojans for ten years, beset by a plague sent by the angry god Apollo. The Greek leader Agamemnon, who has offended Apollo by refusing to return a captured girl, is forced to give up his own prize, the maiden Chryseis, to appease the god. In response, Agamemnon takes the girl Briseis, who is the concubine of the great warrior Achilles, as his own prize.

Achilles, furious at the insult to his honor, withdraws from the fight and sulks in his tent, leaving the Greeks vulnerable to the Trojan army. Despite their best efforts, the Greeks are unable to defeat the Trojans and are pushed back to their ships. In the meantime, Hector, the Trojan prince, kills Patroclus, the closest friend of Achilles, enraging the great warrior.

Achilles finally rejoins the fight and, with the help of the god Zeus, he slays Hector and triumphs over the Trojans. However, in his grief over the loss of his friend, he refuses to return Hector's body to his father, King Priam, for proper burial. After much pleading, Achilles finally relents and allows Priam to take the body back to Troy for a proper funeral.

The poem ends with the burial of Hector and the truce between the Trojans and the Greeks, who agree to end the war and return Helen to her husband in Sparta. The Iliad is a powerful and enduring work of literature that explores the themes of war, honor, and mortality.

The Odyssey by Homer (700 B.C.)

The Odyssey is an epic poem written by the ancient Greek poet Homer. It tells the story of the hero Odysseus and his ten-year journey home after the fall of Troy.

Odysseus, the king of Ithaca and a strong and clever warrior, fights in the Trojan War and is instrumental in the victory of the Greeks. However, on his way home, he angers the sea-god Poseidon, who causes him to face many obstacles and delays.

First, Odysseus and his men are blown off course and end up on the island of the cannibalistic Cyclops, where they narrowly escape with their lives. Next, they face the alluring Sirens, whose beautiful singing lures sailors to their deaths. With the help of wax in their ears, Odysseus and his men are able to resist the temptation and sail past.

After this, they encounter the witch-goddess Circe, who turns some of Odysseus' men into pigs. Odysseus is able to save his men and stay with Circe for a year before continuing on his journey. However, he and his men soon face their greatest challenge: the underworld.

In order to consult the spirit of the prophet Tiresias, Odysseus must travel to the underworld and speak with the dead. With the help of the king of the underworld, he is able to do so and receives important advice for his journey home.

Eventually, after many more adventures and challenges, Odysseus returns to Ithaca, where he is finally reunited with his wife, Penelope, and his son, Telemachus. He must then confront and defeat the suitors who have been vying for Penelope's hand in marriage while he was away.

With the help of Telemachus and the goddess Athena, Odysseus is able to defeat the suitors and reclaim his kingdom. The Odyssey ends with the restoration of order to Ithaca and the reunification of Odysseus' family.

The Aeneid by Virgil (29 B.C.)

The Aeneid is a Latin epic poem written by Virgil in the 1st century BC. It tells the story of Aeneas, a Trojan hero who survived the fall of Troy and journeyed to Italy to become the ancestor of the Romans. The poem begins with the Trojans adrift at sea after their city was destroyed by the Greeks. They are guided by the gods to Italy, where they face many challenges, including a war with the local people and a love triangle involving Aeneas, the Queen of Carthage, and the God of War.

After several years of wandering, Aeneas and his followers settle in Latium and establish a city, which will eventually become Rome. Throughout the poem, Aeneas is torn between his duty to fulfill his destiny and his desire for a peaceful and happy life. He ultimately chooses to fulfill his duty, and with the help of the gods, he establishes a new home for his people.

The Aeneid is a powerful and enduring work of literature that has influenced many other works, including Dante's Divine Comedy and Shakespeare's plays. It is considered one of the greatest works of Latin literature and is still studied and admired today.

The Divine Comedy by Dante Alighieri (1321)

The Divine Comedy is a epic poem written by Dante Alighieri between 1308 and 1321. It is widely considered to be one of the greatest works of world literature. The poem is divided into three parts: Inferno, Purgatorio, and Paradiso.

Inferno is the first part of the poem, and it describes Dante's journey through the nine circles of Hell. The nine circles of Hell are each reserved for a specific type of sin, and Dante encounters various historical and mythical figures who have been condemned to suffer in Hell for eternity.

Purgatorio is the second part of the poem, and it describes Dante's journey through Purgatory. In Purgatory, Dante encounters souls who are being punished for their sins, but who will eventually be able to enter Heaven. Dante is guided through Purgatory by the ancient Roman poet Virgil.

Paradiso is the third and final part of the poem, and it describes Dante's journey through Heaven. In Heaven, Dante meets various figures from history and mythology who have been rewarded for their virtue. Dante is eventually able to enter the divine presence of God, and he experiences a spiritual awakening.

The Divine Comedy is considered to be a masterpiece of world literature for its vivid depiction of the afterlife and its exploration of spiritual themes. It continues to be widely read and studied to this day.

The stories are diverse, ranging from comedic to tragic, and they are told in a variety of styles, including verse and prose. The tales are told in a competition, with the winner receiving a free meal at the tavern when they reach their destination. The characters who tell the tales are a diverse group, including a knight, a nun, a monk, a friar, a miller, a merchant, a clerk, a squire, a doctor, a wife of Bath, a parson, and a pardoner.

Some of the most famous tales in the collection include "The Knight's Tale," "The Wife of Bath's Tale," "The Miller's Tale," and "The Pardoner's Tale." "The Knight's Tale" is a romantic tale of love and chivalry, while "The Wife of Bath's Tale" is a feminist tale that explores the role of women in medieval society. "The Miller's Tale" is a humorous tale of deception and trickery, and "The Pardoner's Tale" is a moral tale about the dangers of greed.

Overall, The Canterbury Tales is a masterful work of literature that showcases Chaucer's talent for storytelling and his ability to create complex and interesting characters. The tales are still widely read and studied today for their insight into medieval society and their enduring themes of love, loyalty, and human foibles.

Leaves of Grass by Walt Whitman (1855)

Leaves of Grass is a collection of poetry by Walt Whitman, first published in 1855. The book is divided into several sections, each containing a variety of poems on different themes. The poetry in Leaves of Grass is notable for its celebration of the individual and the natural world, as well as its use of free verse, which was a relatively new form of poetry at the time.

One of the central themes of Leaves of Grass is the idea of the self and the individual's place in the world. Whitman frequently writes about the importance of the individual and the need for people to embrace their own unique identities. He also explores the interconnectedness of all living things, emphasizing the idea that each person is part of a larger whole.

Another important theme in the book is the celebration of nature and the natural world. Whitman writes about the beauty and majesty of the natural world, and he often uses imagery and metaphors drawn from nature to convey his ideas. He also writes about the importance of being connected to the natural world and the need to appreciate its beauty.

The poetry in Leaves of Grass is also notable for its use of free verse, which was a relatively new form of poetry at the time. Unlike traditional poetry, which is written in strict meter and rhyme, free verse is written in a more natural, conversational style. This allows Whitman to express his ideas more freely and to use language in a more creative and fluid way.

Overall, Leaves of Grass is a seminal work of American literature that explores themes of individuality, nature, and the human experience. Through his use of free verse and his celebration of the individual and the natural world, Whitman has created a powerful and enduring collection of poetry.
